The Light at Onset:
How a Harbor Town Holds Memory

There is a particular quality of February light on the water at Onset that has no name I know of. It arrives sideways, low and serious, settling over the docked boats with something like patience.

Post Feature Image

There is a particular quality of February light on the water at Onset that has no name I know of. It arrives sideways, low and serious, settling over the docked boats with something like patience — as if it has been practicing this arrival for months and finally has it right.

I have been coming to Onset for most of my life. Long enough that the town has stopped being a place I visit and has become, instead, a lens I see through. When I stand at the water's edge in winter, I am never standing there alone. I am standing there with every version of myself who has ever stood in the same spot.

"Every landscape holds the memory of everyone who ever stood in it."

This is something I wrote in my notebook several years ago, in a moment that felt more like discovery than composition. I've been trying to understand it ever since.

The Town That Doesn't Quite Forget

Onset is not a famous place. It does not appear on the kind of lists that draw visitors with purposes — not a destination, exactly, more of an arrival. The harbor is small. The summer crowds are real but not overwhelming. In winter, the seasonal cottages close and the town becomes itself again: the year-rounders, the regulars, the people for whom this is not a getaway but simply home.

What I find here, returning each February, is something I struggle to name without making it sound smaller than it is. A sense of duration. The town carries its own past lightly, without nostalgia — or rather, without the kind of packaged nostalgia that has become a product in other coastal towns. It is simply old. Simply itself.

What the Camera Misses

I carry a camera when I walk the harbor in February. I photograph the boats. I photograph the light. I photograph the way ice forms at the edge of the dock in particular configurations that seem specific to this water, this temperature, this particular version of winter.

The photographs are good. Some of them are very good. And yet they do not capture what I am actually looking at — which is not the boats or the ice or the light, but the accumulation of all the times I have seen these things before. Memory layered over memory, until what I see is something that exists only in my experience of this place, assembled over years.

This is what I have been trying to write about for as long as I have been writing. Not place itself, but what place does to time. How returning to the same spot repeatedly is a kind of argument with memory, a way of testing what we carry against what we find.

The light at Onset in February doesn't need a name, I've decided. It needs only to be returned to. That is the answer the harbor keeps offering, in its patient, sideways way. Come back. Come back. Come back.
